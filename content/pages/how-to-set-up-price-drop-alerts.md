# How to Set Up Price Drop Alerts — Consumer FAQ

Price drop alerts notify you when a product you are tracking drops below a target price. This FAQ covers how to set up, manage, and optimise price drop alerts for the best results.

---

## What Is a Price Drop Alert?

A price drop alert is a notification triggered when a tracked product's price falls to or below a price threshold you set.

Rather than checking a product's price daily, you define:
1. **Which product** to track
2. **At what price** you want to be notified
3. **How to notify you** (email, push notification, SMS)

When any retailer drops the product to your target price, you receive an alert.

---

## How to Set Up a Price Drop Alert

### Step 1: Find the Product You Want to Track

Search for the product on BuyWhere or navigate to a specific product comparison page. Most price tracking platforms offer an "Add alert" or "Track price" button on every product page.

### Step 2: Set Your Target Price

Choose the price at which you want to be notified:

| Alert Type | How It Works |
|------------|-------------|
| **Fixed price target** | Alert triggers at a specific dollar amount (e.g., alert me when this drops to $200) |
| **Percentage off** | Alert triggers when price drops by a set percentage from the current or average price |
| **Below average** | Alert triggers when price falls below the product's historical average |

**Tip**: Set your target price slightly above the lowest price you have seen historically. That way you will catch near-optimal deals without waiting for the absolute floor.

### Step 3: Choose Your Notification Channel

Most platforms offer multiple notification methods:

- **Email**: Alerts sent to your registered email address
- **Push notification**: Instant alerts via mobile app or browser
- **SMS**: Text message alerts (some platforms offer this as a premium feature)

### Step 4: Confirm and Activate

Once you set the product, price target, and notification channel, your alert is live. Most platforms show your active alerts in a dashboard where you can manage them.

## What Is a Good Price Alert Threshold?

Setting the right threshold balances catching good deals against alert fatigue:

### Setting Thresholds Based on Historical Data

If a product's price history shows:
- **Lowest price**: $250
- **Average price**: $320
- **Highest price**: $399

A reasonable alert threshold would be **$270–290** — close to the floor but not so tight that it only triggers on rare deep discounts.

### Threshold Strategies

| Goal | Threshold Approach |
|------|-------------------|
| Catch every discount | Set just below current price |
| Only near-optimal deals | Set at historical low or slightly above |
| Balance of frequency and quality | Set at average price or 10–15% below average |

### Avoid Setting Thresholds Too Tight

If you set a $1 below the current price and the product only fluctuates by $2, you will get an alert. If you set a threshold so tight it never triggers, the alert is useless.

Review your thresholds every few months. If a product's price has trended down permanently, your threshold may be outdated.

## How to Manage Multiple Alerts

### Organise by Category

If you track many products, organise alerts by category:
- Electronics
- Home appliances
- Fashion

This helps you review and adjust thresholds across similar products together.

### Use the Alert Dashboard

Most price tracking platforms provide an alert management view showing:
- Product name and thumbnail
- Your target price
- Current price
- Lowest historical price
- Alert status (active, triggered, paused)

Review this weekly to:
- Pause alerts for products you no longer want
- Lower thresholds for products where prices have stabilised lower
- Delete alerts for products you have already purchased

### Pause vs. Delete

**Pause** an alert when you are temporarily not interested but might want to reactivate later (e.g., you bought an alternative product but might return it).

**Delete** an alert when you no longer care about tracking that product.

## How Do Alerts Work Across Multiple Retailers?

BuyWhere monitors prices across all tracked retailers for a product. When **any** retailer drops to your target price, the alert fires.

This means:
- You do not need separate alerts for each retailer
- You always get the notification for the lowest current price
- If Retailer A has the product at your target price but Retailer B is cheaper, you get both notifications (or just the lowest, depending on your settings)

### Alert Priority

If you want to be notified only when a **specific retailer** drops to your target, some platforms support retailer-specific alerts. Otherwise, alerts are product-level across all retailers.

## Common Alert Configuration Mistakes

### 1. Setting Thresholds Too High

An alert at $1 below the current price will never fire if prices only move $0.50 at a time. Set thresholds based on historical price movement.

### 2. Not Updating Thresholds

If you set an alert when a product launched at $500 and it is now regularly priced at $350, your alert will never trigger. Update thresholds to reflect current market conditions.

### 3. Alerting on Volatile Products

Some products (cryptocurrency hardware, limited-edition sneakers) have extreme price volatility. Short-duration alerts may trigger and then show the price returned to normal within hours. Consider whether a price alert is the right tool for these categories.

### 4. Ignoring Stock Status

An alert might trigger for a product at your target price only to find it out of stock. Some platforms allow filtering alerts to only fire when the product is in stock.

## How to Get the Most Out of Price Alerts

### Combine with Price History

Before setting an alert, review the price history chart:
- Is the product's current price near the historical low, high, or average?
- Does the product follow seasonal price patterns?
- When was the last time it hit your target price?

This helps you set a realistic threshold.

### Set Alerts Before Sale Events

Major sale events (Black Friday, Prime Day) are predictable. Set your alerts a few weeks before these events so you can catch price drops as they happen rather than scrambling after the sale starts.

### Use Multiple Price Points

Some platforms allow stacking multiple alert thresholds on the same product. For example:
- Alert me at $300 (good deal)
- Alert me at $250 (great deal)
- Alert me at $220 (exceptional deal)

This lets you decide in real-time whether a particular discount is worth acting on.

## How to Cancel or Delete an Alert

Most platforms let you manage alerts from your alert dashboard:

1. Navigate to "My Alerts" or "Price Alerts"
2. Find the product you want to stop tracking
3. Click "Delete" or "Remove"

Alternatively, the confirmation email you received when you created the alert typically contains a one-click unsubscribe link.
